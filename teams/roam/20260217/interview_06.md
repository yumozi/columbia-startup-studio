WARM-UP

Interviewer: "Thanks so much for taking the time to chat with me today."
"Yeah totally, this sounded interesting so I figured why not."

CONTEXT / SCREENING

"What does a typical week look like for you outside of school?"

"I’m pretty busy during the week — mostly with recruiting for new grad jobs. But I also help organize events for a group of undergrads from back home, so a lot of my time goes into planning things for other people. Weekends are usually when we try to actually do stuff together, like tusker and chill remember."

"Is there a gap between what you want to do and what you're actually doing?"

"Yeah, definitely. Not because there aren’t things to do — there are too many ideas. It’s more that actually getting people to commit is the hard part."

PROBLEM DISCOVERY

"Last time you saw something online and wanted to do it — what happened?"

"I see stuff all the time on partiful, instagram and the general Kenyans group chat, but honestly it’s just noise at this point. Like I’ll scroll past ten things that look fun, but I don’t even bother saving them unless its for East Africans or sth like that. The problem isn’t ideas, it’s getting people to go, for instance, we're still planning on the swahili village thing, remember."

"Walk me through the last time you tried to plan something with friends."

"I was organizing a group dinner for people from us. I sent a message in the group chat and created the poll, and of course half the people didn’t respond. A few said ‘maybe,’ which basically means no. So I had to follow up individually, like actually texting people and being like ‘are you coming or not.’ It felt like I had to force and annoy people just to get a clear answer."

"What ended up happening?"

"It worked, but only because I pushed, including you. I ended up putting everything on a shared group calendar — date, time, location — and that helped a lot. Once it’s on a calendar, it feels more real, like an actual commitment instead of just a vague idea."

"How often does this kind of coordination happen?"

"Every single time. If you don’t push, nothing happens. People are interested in theory, but no one takes initiative. So either you step up and organize, or the plan just dies."

"What tools are you using right now?"

"WhatsApp group chat mostly for communication, and then Google Calendar for anything serious. The calendar is the only thing that actually gets people to show up."

"What do you like about that setup?"

"The calendar part. It creates accountability. Once something is scheduled, y'all usually show up. At times I wonder if you just ignore the group chat."

"What feels broken?"

"The gap between ‘this sounds fun’ and ‘this is happening.’ There’s no system that bridges that. Everything starts in a casual chat or on Instagram, but it doesn’t translate into an actual plan unless someone manually pushes it through."

DEPTH QUESTIONS

"How do you deal with people being flaky?"

"I call them out, honestly. Not aggressively, but I’ll be like ‘you said you were coming.’ Because otherwise people just drift. There’s no social cost to flaking unless someone enforces it."

"If you could wave a magic wand and fix this, what would change?"

"I’d want something that makes commitment easier and more visible. Like, not just ‘interested’ but actually locking people in. And something that reduces the amount of chasing I have to do. I don’t want to be the person constantly following up."

"So less about finding ideas, more about making them happen?"

"Exactly. Ideas are everywhere. Execution is the problem."

"Would something that recommends new places or events be useful?"

"Not really. I already have more ideas than I can act on. If anything, more recommendations just add more work and stress."
